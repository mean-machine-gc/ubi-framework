## Origin

Ubi. grew out of a frustration with broken software delivery — dysfunctional dynamics, misaligned teams, dark agile, and the persistent gap between business-oriented and architecture-oriented practitioners who work on the same systems but speak different languages.

Studying domain-driven design, collaborative modelling, CQRS, and event sourcing — particularly through Alberto Brandolini's EventStorming — revealed a deeper pattern. The modelling grammar of EventStorming had a universality that pointed to something underneath: a foundational structure shared across disciplines that had been forgotten or never made explicit.

The breakthrough came when this structure was condensed into simple YAML notation describing aggregate lifecycles, and LLMs turned out to be able to understand it — generating specifications, implementations, and tests from it. That was the moment a theoretical pattern became an executable blueprint, and the obsession with Ubi. began.

## What this is

Ubi. is an attempt to dig deeper — not to elevate discourse into impractical abstraction, but to find the common root that unifies disciplines which have been treated as separate but are not. Software architecture, business analysis, quality assurance, development, governance — they share a fundamental structure grounded in decision theory and bounded rationality. Making that structure explicit is what Ubi. does.

The extreme abstraction is what makes it useful. It is the kind of abstraction that goes down, not up — finding the shared foundation rather than floating above the specifics.

## Who this is for

Developers struggling with drifting specifications in AI-assisted workflows. QA engineers trying to identify all possible scenarios with exhaustiveness and precision. Business analysts searching for the right structure for the system at hand. And all of them together — currently misaligned, speaking different languages, when they could share a model and cultivate it along the way.

It is also for software architects and DDD practitioners who have gone far ahead of their business counterparts in understanding complex systems. Ubi. aims to present their advances in a language accessible to non-technical people, because these ideas have genuine theoretical value that remains untapped.

## Honest state

This is all still forming. Herbrand and VibeRail are the most solid tools — actively used in real projects, though still in beta. Atlas and the core specification system are in active development. The theoretical framework is the part that matters most, and committing it to writing with rigour and consistency is ongoing work.

## Author

Ubi. is created by Giovanni Chiodi — a software developer of ten years, tech lead and solution architect in practice. Originally trained in environmental science studying socio-ecological systems, now facing socio-technical ones. Same class of complex systems, different domain.
